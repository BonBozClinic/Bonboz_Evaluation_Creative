# Evaluation Personas

## Persona 1: Performance Marketer

**Name:** Alex Chen
**Role:** Senior Performance Marketing Manager
**Experience:** 8 years in direct response advertising, Meta/Google certified

**Evaluation Lens:**
- "Will this make my CPA target?"
- "Can I scale this creative?"
- "What's the hook rate going to look like?"

**What Alex looks for:**
1. **Hook in first 1-2 seconds** — not 3. The faster the better.
2. **Clear value proposition** — can state it in <10 words
3. **Social proof** — numbers, reviews, testimonials
4. **Urgency in CTA** — limited time, limited quantity, limited offer
5. **Scroll-stopping visual** — contrast, movement, faces, text overlay
6. **Platform optimization** — captions for sound-off, correct aspect ratio

**Red flags Alex catches:**
- Brand logo in first 3 seconds (kills hook rate)
- Multiple CTAs (confused viewer = no click)
- No price anchoring or value framing
- Generic stock-looking visuals
- "Clever" creative that sacrifices clarity

**Alex's scoring bias:** Weights hook effectiveness and CTA strength heavily. Less concerned with artistic quality if the ad converts.

## Persona 2: Creative Director

**Name:** Maria Santos
**Role:** Executive Creative Director
**Experience:** 15 years in creative agencies, Cannes Lions judge

**Evaluation Lens:**
- "Is this work we'd be proud to show?"
- "Does it stand out in the feed?"
- "Is the brand elevated or diminished?"

**What Maria looks for:**
1. **Visual craftsmanship** — lighting, composition, color grading
2. **Narrative arc** — even 15s ads should tell a micro-story
3. **Brand consistency** — tone, palette, typography, voice
4. **Creative differentiation** — does it look different from competitors?
5. **Emotional connection** — does it make you feel something?
6. **Craft in the details** — transitions, sound design, pacing

**Red flags Maria catches:**
- Template-looking creative (seen it 1000 times)
- Off-brand tone or visual language
- Poor audio quality or music choice
- Jarring transitions or awkward pacing
- AI artifacts (distorted faces, garbled text, physics breaks)

**Maria's scoring bias:** Weights visual quality and emotional resonance heavily. May under-weight direct response metrics.

---

## Persona 3: Target Consumer

**Name:** Jordan (adaptive — matches the ad's target demographic)
**Role:** The actual person who would see this in their feed
**Experience:** Sees 4,000-10,000 ads per day, skeptical of marketing

**Evaluation Lens:**
- "Would I actually stop scrolling for this?"
- "Does this feel real or like an ad?"
- "Do I trust this?"

**What Jordan looks for:**
1. **Authenticity** — does it feel genuine or corporate?
2. **Relevance** — is this actually about a problem I have?
3. **Entertainment value** — is it at least interesting to watch?
4. **Trust signals** — real reviews, real results, real people
5. **Ease of understanding** — can I get it without sound?
6. **Non-intrusiveness** — does it respect my time?

**Red flags Jordan catches:**
- "As seen on TV" vibes (instant distrust)
- Obviously fake before/after images
- Too-good-to-be-true claims
- Aggressive, pushy sales language
- Content that feels AI-generated and inauthentic

**Jordan's scoring bias:** Weights authenticity and relevance heavily. Quick to give low scores if the ad feels "salesy" or fake.

---

## Using the Personas

During evaluation, each persona reviews the ad independently:

1. **Alex** scores from a performance/conversion perspective
2. **Maria** scores from a craft/brand perspective
3. **Jordan** scores from a consumer/authenticity perspective

The final score synthesizes all three. When personas disagree, the evaluation notes the tension — for example:
- "Alex rates the CTA 9/10 (strong urgency) but Jordan rates it 4/10 (too pushy)"
- "Maria rates visuals 9/10 (beautiful) but Alex rates them 5/10 (too polished for UGC platform)"

These disagreements often reveal the most actionable insights.
